Let's start with the simplest or the part that always appears in a subject or object (you'll know what those are much later): The Noun.

When you're discussing about any topic, you need to label components of it inorder to identify them uniquely. It allows you to differentiate between each part and allows you to relate a part to another part. This label is a Noun. It identifies things.

N.B. All information presented cannot be completely presented through the perspective of analogy. Well it could be, but it would be less useful than presenting them as raw. For example, how it would not be much useful to explain addition itself but show its application and understand it through its use cases.

We begin by casting a net of the broadest category and then dig deeper into each section.

1. Broad Nouns

There are 4 types of nouns:

  • People
  • Places
  • Things
  • Ideas

1.1. People

This kind of nouns usually refers to names of human being. It could be animals or any living things. 

This is _Astri_.

"Astri" is the name of a person.

1.2. Places

These nouns refers to a location. It doesn't have to be with-in Earth or even this galaxy. The place could even be outside of this Universe (if you are the chosen one who has a special pass to travel between universes) 

Astri is originally from _Malang_.

"Malang" is the name of a place, a city in Indonesia.

1.3. Things

This refers to in-animate items. They refer to something which is not a living thing, but can be found tangibly in nature. 

She likes the _cat_.

"Cat" refers to the generic breed of ferocious little tigers who have domesticated us humans. It's considered a Noun, actually a specific category with-in it: a Common Noun.

1.4. Ideas

Last, these are nouns which refer to the intangible items which has a hidden meaning in it. 

Luccan has big _dreams_.

"Dreams" is a word which exists. People 'have' them, long for it, are unable to touch it, but some people manifest it. However, most people can't remember it. Some also continously chase it.

2. Narrow Nouns

Another way to categorize the nouns is by tangibility / physicality and generality / specificity. The first category is between a Concrete Noun vs Abstract Noun. The latter is between Common Noun vs Proper Noun.

2.1. Concrete

These are nouns which you interact with physically. 

A dog chases a cat.

A "dog" and "cat" are both concrete nouns. From the broader category of nouns, these nouns generally fall into: People, Places or Things.

2.2. Abstract

Nouns which are not-physical; they exist as an Idea. 

Humans experience sadness.

"sadness" is not something you can touch. It implies a certain meaning but it only exists as an idea with in our human mind. It can't be measured (it's not quantitate yet).

2.3. Common

A category of nouns which categorizes a group of things and gives them a common label to denote them. 

A dog climbs a mountain.

Both "dog" and "mountain" are generic terms to denote a specific group.

2.4. Proper

Proper nouns are particular about naming. They focus within the group for identification. 

The dog, Harry, climbs mount Kilimanjaro.

"Harry" and "Kilimanjaro" are specific types of dog and mountain respectively. One thing about proper nouns is that they must be capitalized in a sentence.

3. More than One

As with many other things, there are more than one item of a thing. Naturally, nouns adapt to name them as well. The name of this category is trivial: Singular and Plural Nouns.

But be slightly warned though. The Plural form of Nouns are anything but trivial. It has its own several different subcategories. Anways, let's start with the Singular form:

3.1. Singular

Singular nouns are simple: It's one of a thing. For example, 

_Astri_ is a singular being.

"Astri" is a single person.

3.2. Plural

When nouns refer to multiple things, they take this form. 

Astri is among many _girls_ i have met.

"girls" refer to many of the single entity: a girl.

However, and there is going to be a lot of 'howevers', English grammar likes to explore new ways of doing things. Yes, there is a category with-in plural nouns itself: Regular and Irregular.

3.2.1. Regular

For regular nouns, pluralization is easy: append 's' at the end of the word. To rehash the previous example, 

Astri is among many _girls_ i have met.

"girls" is the regular plural of the singular form: girl.

3.2.2. Irregular

Irregular nouns are so named because there are many of them with their unique origin and forms

  1. ending in en

  2. base plurals

    Nouns which has the same form as its Singular. 

    |----------+----------|
| Singular | Plural   |
|----------+----------|
| fish     | fish     |
| deer     | deer     |
| aircraft | aircraft |
| sheep    | sheep    |
| rice     | rice     |
|----------+----------|

    That's the general rule. And the possible reason they are this way because they are seen as a collective group which are "uncountable" in nature. Why this interpretaion?

    For example, you can definitely count grains of rice, however, its tedious; it is far easier to think of it as a collective item instead. Same analogy goes for the other examples listed above. In a sentence, examples would be 

    bison migrate west # singular
bison migrates west # plural

    However, there are exceptions. Let's use the word 'fish' as an example. One uses the regular plural form when they denoting something countable instead of the "collective". For example, 

    martha studies 30 fishes

    "fishes" denotes different species of fishes. And different species are countable where as, 

    marty caught 30 fish.

    "fish" denotes plural. Although it's counted, it can be taken as a collective in comparison to "fishes".
